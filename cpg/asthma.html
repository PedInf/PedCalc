Pediatric Asthma

DEFINITION

Chronic inflammatory condition of the lung airways resulting in episodic airflow obstruction

DIAGNOSIS

  • No standard test to diagnose asthma, pulmonary function test can help a lot if the child can perform it (usually above 6 years)
  • The presence of intermittent dry cough and expiratory wheezes that respond to bronchodilators with the exclusion of other risk factors in a child is the mainstay of asthma diagnosis
  • For children aged 5 years or less, where lung function tests cannot be effectively carried out, differential diagnoses should be given careful consideration, including but not limited to, assessment of the following:
  1. Allergic rhinitis and allergic sinusitis
  2. Obstructions involving large airways
  3. Obstructions involving small airways
  4. Viral bronchiolitis or obliterative bronchiolitis
  5. Cystic fibrosis
  6. Bronchopulmonary dysplasia
  7. Heart disease
  8. Medication induced

Any of the following symptoms must be considered for diagnosis of asthma in children:

  1. Recurrent breathlessness, chest tightness, wheezing or cough.
  2. Often worse at night and early morning.
  3. Signs of airway obstruction.
  4. Symptoms follow viral RTI, exercise, emotions or exposure to aeroallergens or irritants.
  5. Improve with bronchodilators and anti-inflammatory therapy.
  6. Absence of seasonal variation in wheeze.
  7. Personal history of atopy.
  8. Wheeze heard on auscultation.
  9. Improvements of symptoms or lung function in response to adequate therapy.
  10. Presence of one major risk factor (parental history of asthma or eczema) or two of three minor risk factors (eosinophilia, wheezing without colds, and allergic rhinitis).

Asthma exacerbation management Action plan

Level of severity Action
Mild Manage at ER and discharge, unless the case shifted to more severe level.
Moderate Manage at ER and reassess the need for admission by Pediatrician.
Severe Admit to Pediatric ward
Life- threatening Admit to ICU
  • Moderate and above should be managed by Pediatric staff.
  • A patient may move between levels of asthma severity whilst receiving treatment.  It is important to be continually aware of indications that severity level has changed.
  • Continually refer to assessed of asthma severity

The patient should have most of the lower severity level features to be shifted from one severity to the milder one.

MILD SEVERITY MANAGEMENT

  • Inhaled SABA by nebulizer (or MDI with valved holding chamber) ipratropium bromide Oxygen, up to 3 doses in first hour.
    • SABA is superior to ipratropium (see notes)
  • Oral systemic corticosteroids if no immediate response or if patient recently took oral systemic corticosteroids.
  • Reassess after each nebulizer.
  • For doses see the table Dosages of Drugs for Asthma Exacerbations.
  • Continue treatment 13 hours, provided there is improvement.
  • Make admission decision in less than 4 hours
  • If there is good response:
    • Discharge home.
    • Continue treatment with inhaled SABA.
    • Continue course of oral systemic corticosteroid.
    • Consider initiation of an ICS.
  • Patient education:
    • Review medications, including inhaler technique.
    • Review predisposing factors.
    • Recommend follow up with pediatrician within 1 week.

MODERATE SEVERITY MANAGEMENT

  • Start with the following medications:
    • Salbutamol (neb) every 2hr Oxygen.
    • Steroids (IV or PO).
    • Hydration.
  • Assess after 4 hours.
    • If the child is not improving follow the severe management.
    • If the child is improving:
      • Decrease frequency of nebulizers gradually by increasing 1 hour each time to ≥4 hourly.
      • Assess each 6 hour.
        • If the child is not improving:
          • Continue on present treatment.
          • Add Ipratropium (nebulizer).
          • Search for predisposing cause.
          • Consider other diagnosis.
        • If the child is improving:
          • Apply discharge criteria, if fulfilled, discharge patient.

SEVERE SEVERITY MANAGEMENT

  • Start with the following medications:
    • Oxygen.
    • Salbutamol (nebulizer) hourly.
    • Steroids (IV/PO).
    • Ipratropium (nebulizer).
    • Hydration.
  • Monitor SpO2, if <95% in room air, do blood gases.
  • Chest X-ray.
  • Assess after 3 hrs.
    • If the child is not improving
      • consider advanced management like magnesium sulphate
      • consider communicating with pediatric pulmonologist and transferring the patient
    • If the child is improving:
      • Decrease frequency of nebulizers gradually by increasing 1 hour each time to ≥2hourly.
      • Assess each 3 hours.
      • If the child continues to improve manage as moderate severity.

DISCHARGE CRITERIA

  • Severity assessment not above mild level
  • Patient on no more frequently than 4th hourly inhaled bronchodilator and off supplemental oxygen
  • Pulse Oximetry 95% or above
  • Normal feeding for age no dehydration or IV fluid needs
  • Appropriate timing of discharge i.e., social factors favorable, parents/family able to care for child at home.

DISCHARGE PLAN

  • Parent & Patient education
  • Environmental control measures (predisposing factors)
  • Review medications, their benefits and side effects
  • Inhaler technique demonstration
  • Home exacerbation management
  • Supply medications and devices
  • Organize and explain follow up with Pediatrician
  • Asthma discharge summary is complete
  • Asthma Management Plan provided to the family

Notes:
  • There is no known advantage for higher doses of corticosteroids in severe asthma exacerbations, nor is there any advantage for intravenous administration over oral therapy provided gastrointestinal transit time or absorption is not impaired.
  • The total course of systemic corticosteroids for an asthma exacerbation requiring an Emergency Room visit of hospitalization may last from 3 to 10 days.
  • For corticosteroid courses of less than 1(one) week, there is no need to taper the dose. For slightly longer courses (e.g., up to 10 days), there probably is no need to taper, especially if patients are concurrently taking ICSs.
  • ICSs can be started at any point in the treatment of an asthma exacerbation.

OUTPATIENT MANAGEMENT OF ASTHMA FOR CHILDREN


NOTES

  • Step down should be done only if the patient controlled for 3 and above months
  • Patients on salbutamol inhaler only should have no limitations of activities, no Nocturnal or daytime symptoms
  • ICS (inhaled steroids) are superior to LTRA: Leukotriene Receptor Antagonist used as second step therapy
  • LTRA can be beneficial in patients with comorbid allergic rhinitis, young children with recurrent viral-induced exacerbations, or children with exercise-induced symptoms
  • long-acting beta agonists (LABAs) is adjuvant therapy and should not be used alone in pediatrics
  • the use of LABAs below 5 years of age not fully studied
  • Theophylline
  • has a limited role in the treatment of childhood asthma
  • If alternative treatment is used and response is inadequate, discontinue it and use the preferred treatment before stepping up.
  • If clear benefit is not observed within 46 weeks and patient/family medication technique and adherence are satisfactory, consider adjusting therapy or alternative diagnosis.
  • Before diagnosis of Cough variant asthma rule out post nasal drip (chronic upper airway cough syndrome), allergic rhinitis, gastroesophageal reflux (GERD) and vocal cord dysfunction

FOLLOW-UP OF PATIENT ON INHALED STEROIDS

  • Low- to medium-dose ICS
    • Monitor blood pressure and weight with each physician visit
    • Measure height annually; monitor periodically for declining growth rate and pubertal developmental delay
    • Encourage regular physical exercise
    • Ensure adequate dietary calcium and vitamin D with additional supplements for daily calcium if needed
    • Avoid smoking
    • Ensure TSH status if patient has history of thyroid abnormality
  • High-dose ICS, at least 4 courses oral corticosteroid/yr
    • Yearly ophthalmologic evaluations to monitor for cataracts or glaucoma
    • Baseline bone densitometry (DEXA scan)
    • Consider patient at increased risk for adrenal insufficiency, especially with physiologic stressors (e.g., surgery, accident, significant illness)
